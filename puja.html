GOSPEL OF PUJA
Posted by Admin
Day 0

Tomorrow we leave.

Vera held a final briefing tonight, and I spent most of it watching the condensation coil behind her shoulder. The thing’s been dripping irregularly lately—Lir says it's just surface tension differences from the pressure cycling. Still, makes me nervous. Reminds me how nothing here is really built to last.

We’ve been given one of the old RV’s from the extraction days. Lir’s been retrofitting the wheels and patched up the solar shielding. Says it’ll make the trip, “if God wills it.”

Inventory’s done. Food will run tight before we’re halfway back—assuming we even make it that far. Water’s better, for we’re praying.

Jerrica’s quiet tonight. She’s been scanning over fungal genomes again. She always did think the future could be bred into people.

Armando blessed the RV this morning, pressed a rough palm to its undercarriage and murmured something in Spanish under his breath. I envy that.

King is asleep. He doesn’t know we’re leaving, but he’s going. Jerrica insisted. Not sure if it’s sentiment or specimen interest. The little guy’s shedding. New scales look slightly iridescent.

I’m bringing the field kit, two flasks of methanol, a burner. It’s from Earth. I’ve been saving it.

They say Mars is silent. But I’ve learned: it hums. Under the soil, inside the machines, in your own head after a while.

Tomorrow we go west to find something that might save the colony. I think I’m okay with it.

Day 1

We left at 07:48 MTC. Forty-eight minutes late because Lir’s power diagnostics glitched on boot, and Vera insisted we run a full backup cycle. “Just in case,” she said. Just in case what, I know—radio works for now, and God knows there’s one on this planet waiting to pick up our black box.

We’ve started west, into the red flatlands. Not much to look at—just ochre dust, shale fragments, and the odd iron outcrop. Armando’s already taken five samples. Or rather, Armando has already licked 5 random rocks. He gets this gleam in his eye when he sees a ridge line like it’s flirting with him. I said as much.

Jerrica hasn’t said much. She’s got that distant look again, scanning growth models like she’s going to summon a miracle out of decimal points.

Nik asked to drive. Vera said Smart. The kid nearly dropped our only medkit trying to “reorganize” the supplies. Still, they’ve got that optimism. Kept trying to name the RV something dumb, like Big Girl or The Womb Truck. I laughed—I think.

We covered about 40 kilometers before nightfall. We’ll pick up more distance once we’re past the broken terrain. Vera’s keeping us on tight shifts. I’m in the 2nd nav seat, mostly watching for rock fall and radiation spikes. I’ve logged 17 already, all within tolerable levels. For now.

Dinner was rehydrated starch blocks with salt tabs. King ate better. Jerrica fed him a cricket frozen from the colony’s gene bank. He chirped. Everyone acted like that was cute. I don’t want to scream.

We’ll sleep inside tonight, packed like cargo. I brought earplugs. Still hear the hum.

Maybe I came out here hoping for something to fail in a way I could finally stop pretending to fix. But everyone keeps holding it together.

And we’re one day in.

Day 8

The water filter broke.

Technically, it’s the secondary pressure valve that cracked—but the effect is the same: no filtration, no clean water. Lir spent two hours trying to weld it back together with a filament tip and whatever scrap he could cannibalize from the rover’s spare hull lining. It held for twenty-three minutes before bursting with a noise like a bone snapping underwater.

We still have reserves. Vera says we’re fine for now, but everyone’s doing the math in their heads.

Jerrica lost it a bit. Quietly. She didn’t yell or anything — just... got that look, like someone was peeling her open from the inside. She spent the afternoon with King, misting his enclosure manually with a syringe. Said it’s calming. I can’t tell if she meant King or herself.

Nik offered to take shorter water rations. Said they “don’t need that much anyway.” I think they mean it. I think they believe that martyring themselves is a kind of competence. That’s the part—how pure it all still is with them. I know to admire them for it.

Armando found a basalt ridge and wanted to sample it. Vera said no. He didn’t argue, but I saw him looking back at it through the window for a long time. I think he prays when he thinks no one’s watching. I caught him murmuring to his rock hammer like it was a rosary.

Anyway. No one’s dead.

Day 10

Nik found water.

That should feel like a miracle.

We were parked near the edge of a canyon shelf when they slipped. Went chasing a topographic anomaly on the rover’s scanner—said it looked “wrong, but wrong in a good way.” Vera told them to wait, but they were already halfway down the slope before the words finished forming.

I thought they’d broken something. We all did. They didn’t answer the first three hails. Jerrica looked like she was about to throw up. Then the comm crackled—static, then Nik’s voice, breathless, like they were laughing and choking at the same time.

“There’s ice,” they said. “There’s f—frozen water, tucked into a little wedge under a thermal shelf. I think we can melt it.”

The relief in the RV was… grotesque. Like someone had just reset the death clock. Lir jumped into action immediately, started prepping a siphon and filtration line. Armando said “blessed be,” unironically. Vera smiled. A real smile, for the first time in days. I just sat there. Watching her.

It was like we were all in a play and I’d forgotten my lines.

Nik got back covered in red dust, bleeding from a scratch down their left arm, and beaming. They looked like a kid who’d found treasure. Vera hugged them. I thought for a second Jerrica might cry. She didn’t. Instead she gave them her protein ration. Again. That’s twice now.

How quickly everyone snapped back into belief—this solved something. We found water.

Nik is still smiling in the corner. I want to ask them why.

I want to ask what they think is waiting at the end of this road.

Day 15
It came from the south, fast. Faster than it should have. Lir saw the drop in barometric pressure before anyone else—shouted for us to seal the vents, stow what we could, and brace. Armando yelled something about dust devils, but this wasn’t a devil. It was a god. A furious, shapeless god of grit and static and wind. The sky turned orange-black. Visibility dropped to meters. The RV started to scream—not the engine, the body. The metal itself flexing under pressure, joints groaning like something alive was trying to crawl out from inside. We bailed. No choice. Nik spotted a rock face up ahead, maybe fifty meters east—called out a shadow that turned out to be a cave. A real one. Not a scan blip, not a rover pit stop—an actual pocket in the Martian crust, carved by pressure and time. We made a call. No choice, really. If the RV rolled, we were dead. We had to get it under cover. So we dragged it. Harnesses, tethers, shoulders to metal. Jerrican and Armando pushed from the rear while Vera and Lir guided the front. I ran alongside, calling slope angles and surface types. Nik lit the path with a flare. Every meter was war. The wind ripped at our suits. Static popped in our ears. The ground kept sliding out beneath us. At one point we almost lost Jerrica in a side-drift, but Very pulled her back by the wrist just in time. It took us 27 minutes. I counted. And then — somehow — we got the RV into the cave. Inside, it’s cold. Bitter. And quiet in a way that doesn't feel safe. The storm outside sounds like a war between planets. Nik managed to calibrate the long-range transmitter and sent an SOS ping. Repeated bursts, tagged to Colony East. Vera dictated it: "Storm event, loss of visibility, possible shelter coordinates. Request confirmation.” We waited. For hours. Then the tower blew. It was subtle—a snap, a smoke curl, a final death whine from the circuit boards. Nik punched the cave wall. Didn’t say anything after that. We are, as of now, alone. Jerrica hasn’t spoken since. She’s just sitting with King, wiping dust from his tank every fifteen minutes like it’s the one thing she can control. Lir keeps checking the fried tower, muttering possibilities. Vera’s trying to keep us on a rotation schedule, assigning watches and water duties like she still believes someone is coming. I wish I believed someone was. We don’t know if the colony got the ping. We don’t know if they think we’re dead.
Day 18
Today broke something. Not the radio—though that’s still dead. Not the cave walls—though the wind hasn’t stopped screaming. Something else cracked. Maybe us. It started with the water again. It always circles back to water. Lir was trying to re-rig the filtration line Nik pulled from the rover, but it’s not designed for this. We’ve been boiling meltwater in the fuel basin, scraping mineral dust from the edges, trying not to breathe too deep. Nik laughed. That made it worse. “I found the water,” they said, like that settled it. And maybe it should’ve. But Lir shot back, something sharp and technical about inefficiencies and contamination and how finding something doesn’t mean much if it can’t be used. Vera tried to mediate, but even she couldn’t keep the tone out of her voice. Nik went quiet, eyes glassy, body eerily still. After an eternity, they made eye contact with each of us and ran down further into the cave. I didn’t defend them. I didn’t defend anyone. I just sat against the wall, listening to everyone say things we won’t come back from. Lir threw a wrench at the cave wall. Didn’t say anything else for hours. No one made a plan today. We didn’t even count rations. We just yelled until we were tired, then sat in the quiet with our own thoughts, which might be louder. Me? I’m too tired to be afraid. I think part of me always knew it would end like this—dust in the lungs, silence on the radio, and too many people in too small a cave. I keep imagining what it would sound like if the cave collapsed. Probably quieter than today.
Day 20
Nik is hurt. But of course, Nik also found more water. Bleed. Save us. Repeat. They went deeper into the cave system early this morning, before rotation started. Didn’t tell anyone. Vera woke up and realized they were gone—went full command-mode, already halfway into her suit before the rest of us were even conscious. I didn’t rush. Not because I didn’t care, but because… I don’t know. You can only sprint toward someone so many times before it starts to feel like theater. It took three hours to find them. Lir and Armando tracked their boot pattern through a side tunnel until the prints blurred into dust. The air thinned. Jerrica wanted to turn back. Vera didn’t. I followed. We found Nik at the bottom of a basalt chute, wedged between a jagged outcrop and the ice wall they’d apparently been trying to sample. Right leg twisted, bleeding down one side. Their mask was cracked, but sealed enough. And they were smiling again. “I found it,” they said, through a broken tooth. “There’s a whole sheet of ice behind the wall. Thick, too. Cold like Earth winters.” Then they passed out. Vera carried them back herself. Didn’t let Lir help. I think she needed to do it—for control, or guilt, or both. Nik is sleeping now, with King perched on their chest like a paperweight. Jerrica hasn’t said a word to them. She also hasn’t left the med kit’s side since we returned. Lir tried to fix the secondary transmitter again tonight. No luck. The main radio’s still fried. No ping from the colony. So that’s it. Nik’s leg is probably fractured. We’re deeper in the cave than planned. We’re almost out of antibiotics. And we found ice. Enough to buy us weeks. Maybe longer if we ration. But no one smiled about it this time. Even Vera just nodded. We all know what it means now. We’re not going back. Not really. We’re only going forward. It’s cold tonight. Colder than I’ve felt since we left base. I don’t think it’s the air.
Day 21
The silence woke me. Not the shriek of wind against rock, not sand hissing through crevices in the cave wall, not Lir swearing under his breath while recalibrating sensors in the dark. Just silence. It took me a full minute to register it was real. Another to believe it wasn’t worse. Then Vera unsealed the thermal panel covering the entrance. Light poured in—thin, watery, Martian—but after six days underground, it felt like a flood. We stepped outside like ghosts. Everyone blinking, squinting, silent. The horizon was clean. No walls of rust, no smoke-thick sky. Just the stillness that comes after fury. Nik hobbled out last, leaning on Armando like it was nothing. They looked… smaller. But also impossible to ignore. The way everyone looked at them today felt different. Like they knew. Like we all knew. We spent most of the morning salvaging what we could from the RV. The satellite relay is gone. Blasted or buried. Either way, it’s over. The colony thinks we’re dead. Maybe they need to believe that. Jerrica’s stopped asking if we’ll get another message. Lir doesn’t talk about the comms anymore. Vera stares off when no one’s speaking. She’s doing the math. We packed up and left the cave around mid-afternoon. The dust makes it hard to gauge time, but it felt right. The land ahead is smoother now, less elevation, easier for Nik to manage. Armando plotted a course northeast, following the slope of the last ridge. Eastward. Always eastward. The storm ended. But it didn’t bring relief. Just clarity. We are well and truly alone.
Day 25
We started rationing today. Officially. There were mutterings before—Lir running silent calorie counts on his pad, Jerrica turning over nutrient packs in her hands like she could multiply them through will alone—but today, Vera made the call. One pack per person per day. Half if we can stretch it. It’s not enough. Not for this terrain, not for the cold. But it’s all we have. Nik hasn’t complained. They never do. They’ve been walking better—still limping, but moving without help. I caught them whispering to King last night, something about Earth lizards and heat lamps. The way they talk to that creature… it’s gentle. I don’t see that in the others anymore. Maybe never did. Armando said grace at dinner. Well, whatever counts as dinner now: one squeeze of grainpaste and a handful of filtered melt. I don’t believe in God—never did—but for a second, I was jealous of him. Not of the faith, exactly. Of the shape of it. Something to speak into the silence. Lir made another attempt at a directional ping this morning. Still nothing. “Maybe the colony did try to send something,” he said, like that should comfort us. Maybe they did. Maybe they didn’t. Maybe it doesn’t matter anymore. Everyone’s thinner. Jerrica’s cheeks are drawn. Vera’s fingernails are cracked. Armando hasn’t laughed in four days.
Day 27
RV’s stuck. Of course it is. Which means we have to walk wherever we wanna go. In our hot, ugly spacesuits. I’m not even sure why I’m still keeping this diary. We’re gonna die. Mars is gonna die. I’m not even properly yelling into the void, just wasting my little time left on this wretched planet. Probably gonna be my last entry.
Day 35
I know I said I was done with this damned diary, but I lied. We have found our savior. It’s a fungus — Mons Valley Fungus is our name for it. Thrives on biomass. I’m no energy scientist, but I’ve been told that we could solve our colony’s energy problem if we could capture even a small amount of the energy it gives off. One problem — we’re still stuck here with no means of transportation and no way to get help from the colony. We’re also almost out of power, and once we’re out, we’re dead. Lir and Jerrica think that we could harvest some of the energy from the fungus, and they’re working together on some sort of apparatus for it. But to recharge our batteries, we would need a significant amount of biomass, more than we have just laying around.
Day 38
The fungus—God, we still don’t even know what to call it—it pulses when we get close. Not like it’s hostile. More like it’s waiting. It’s not just eating—it’s responding. The structure’s denser now, almost vascular. The light it gives off is warmer, more rhythmic. Alive. Vera’s the only one still talking like we have choices. She keeps saying we’ll find another way. That we’re scientists. That sacrifice is for myths and martyrs. But even her voice is smaller now. Even she won’t meet Nik’s eyes. It’s not just survival. It’s math. It’s biology. It’s reality. Nik knows that it’ll be them. I think they do. I told myself I didn’t care if this colony died. That I didn’t care if we all failed. But now that it’s in front of me—salvation in a bloom, and the cost standing next to it—I realize I do care.
Day 40
We all knew what was coming. Vera tried to delay it, give speeches about ethics and civilization and the meaning of consent. But that kind of language feels brittle now. We’re past speeches. It took three days of silence shaped like talking. Then this morning she didn’t stop it. They blinked a few times, then they walked away. I think that hurt Jerrica, though she wouldn’t admit it. Vera hasn’t spoken since. She sat outside the camp all night, helmet off, just staring into the fungus-lit dark. Nik sacrificed themselves willingly. That they walked into the bloom for all of us. That they were brave. Nik sacrificed themself.
